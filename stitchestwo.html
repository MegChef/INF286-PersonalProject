Intermediate Stitches

Moss Stitch (Granite Stitch)

The moss stitch crochet stitch is made up of two of your basic crochet stitches. The single crochet stitch and the chain stitch. Through using these two easy stitches, you can make the textured moss stitch. It goes by many names, the granite stitch and woven stitch to name a few.

Here is how to make an Moss stitch:

Foundation Row:
- Ch an even number of stitches (you can choose how many stitches you'd like to start with, but it should be even to keep the stitch pattern symmetrical).
- Example: Ch 20 (or any even number).
Row 1:
- Skip the first ch (this will give you the height of your first sc).
- In the next ch, make a sc.
- Ch 1, then skip the next ch and make another sc in the next ch.
- Repeat this [ch 1, skip 1 ch, sc in next ch] across the row.
- Finish with a sc in the last chain. (You will have a pattern of single crochets with chain-1 spaces between them).
Row 2 and onward:
- Turn your work.
- Ch 1 to begin the new row.
- In the first space between the single crochet stitches of the previous row, make a sc.
- Ch 1, then make a sc in the next chain-1 space from the previous row.
- Repeat this pattern of [ch 1, sc in the next space] across the row.
- End the row with a sc in the last stitch.

Granny Stripe Stitch

This stitch consists of the double crochet stitch, the single crochet stitch and the slip stitch. A crochet stitch guide might be helpful when you come across this stitch. It’s most commonly used in patterns on how to crochet a blanket.

How to Make a Granny Stripe Stitch:

Foundation:
- Ch any multiple of 3 plus 4 extra chains. (For example, if you want to start with 24 stitches for a smaller project, you would ch 28. The extra chains create the turning chain for the double crochet in the next row).
- Example: Ch 28 (or any multiple of 3 + 4).
Row 1 (Foundation Row):
- Dc into the 5th chain from the hook (this is your first double crochet stitch, and the 4 chains at the beginning count as your first double crochet).
- Ch 2, skip 2 chains, and make a dc into the next chain.
- Ch 2, skip 2 chains, and make another dc in the next chain. Repeat this pattern (ch 2, skip 2, dc in the next) across the row.
- At the end of the row, you should end with a dc in the last chain (before your turning chain).
Row 2 (and all following rows):
- Turn your work.
- Ch 3 (this counts as your first double crochet).
- In the next space (the space between two double crochets from the previous row), make a dc.
- Ch 2, then make another dc in the same space (this creates a corner).
- Continue with [ch 2, dc in next space] across the row until you reach the last space.
- At the end of the row, make a dc into the top of the turning chain (the 3rd chain) from the previous row.

































Elizabeth Stitch

The Elizabeth stitch is one of those really unique crochet stitches. It’s also been called the mini bean stitch because of the raised rounded shapes the stitches make. It looks intimidating but it’s really easy to learn and is absolutely beautiful.

How to Make a Elizabeth Stitch:

Foundation Row (Chain Row):
- Start with a foundation chain that is a multiple of 2, plus 1 extra stitch for turning.
- For example, if you want 20 stitches, chain 21.
Row 1 (Foundation Row):
- Turn your work, and in the second chain from the hook, make a single crochet (SC) stitch.
- Continue making single crochets across the row until you reach the last stitch. (Note: This is the foundation row, so just use basic single crochet stitches for this row).
Row 2 (First Elizabeth Stitch Row):
- Turn your work.
- Chain 2 (this will count as your first half of the Elizabeth Stitch).
- Skip the first stitch (the first one is the turning chain), and then in the next stitch, make a half double crochet (HDC).
- Now, in the same stitch where you just did the half double crochet, make a single crochet (SC).
- Skip the next stitch (similar to the previous step) and repeat the process by making a half double crochet followed by a single crochet in the next stitch.
- Repeat this sequence until you reach the last stitch.
- For the final stitch, finish with a half double crochet (HDC).
Row 3 (Repeat Row 2):
- Turn your work, and repeat Row 2 for each additional row.
- Every time you work this stitch pattern, the Elizabeth Stitch creates a nice textured pattern that alternates between half double crochet and single crochet stitches, creating a raised look.

Star Stitch

Start the first star:
- Yarn over and insert your hook into the next stitch.
- Yarn over again and pull up a loop (you should have three loops on your hook).
- Yarn over again and insert your hook into the same stitch again.
- Yarn over and pull up another loop (you should now have 5 loops on your hook).
- Yarn over and insert your hook into the same stitch one more time.
- Yarn over and pull up a loop (you should now have 7 loops on your hook).
Now, close the star:
- Yarn over and pull through all 7 loops on your hook.
- Chain 1 to secure the stitch.
Repeat the Star Stitch:
- Skip the next 2 stitches, and repeat the process to make another star stitch in the next stitch.
- Continue this pattern (skip 2 stitches, make a star stitch) across the row.
End the Row:
- After completing the last star stitch, single crochet into the last stitch of the row to help keep the edge neat.
Subsequent Rows:
- Turn your work, and in each row, repeat the same process:
- For the first star stitch, you will work into the chain-1 space of the previous row's star stitch.
- Continue making star stitches and skipping 2 stitches across the row.
- Be sure to finish each row with a single crochet in the last stitch.


The star stitch creates a beautiful star or flower shapes in the fabric. It's condensed and squishy. One of the most perfect crochet stitches for blankets. It would look beautiful in a baby’s or child’s room.

How to Make a Star Stitch:

Foundation Row (Chain Row):
- Start with a foundation chain that is a multiple of 6, plus 1 extra stitch.
- For example, if you want to make 18 stitches, you would chain 19.
Row 1 (Single Crochet Row):
- Turn your work and single crochet (SC) into the second chain from the hook, and continue single crocheting across the entire row.
Row 2 (Star Stitch Row):
- Turn your work.

Puff Stitch

The puff stitch is so much fun to work up. As far as unique crochet stitches go, this one is super easy too. It creates soft puffs on your fabric. Like mini clouds. This texture looks great in scarves or blankets. I even think it would make a sweater more modern.

How to Make a Puff Stitch:

  1. Yarn Over: Yarn over (YO) and insert your hook into the stitch or space where you want to create the puff stitch.
  2. Pull Up a Loop: Yarn over again and pull up a loop. You should now have three loops on your hook.
  3. Repeat: Repeat this process (yarn over, insert hook, pull up a loop) 3-4 times (depending on how puffy you want the stitch). You'll have 5-7 loops on your hook at the end of this step.
  4. Close the Puff: Yarn over and pull through all the loops on your hook. This will close the puff stitch and form the puffy texture.
  5. Chain 1 (optional): Some patterns may ask you to chain 1 to secure the puff stitch. This step helps to separate puff stitches for a more defined look.

Shell Stitch

If you’re looking for one of the most unique crochet stitches, try out the shell stitch. It’s so simple to learn to crochet this stitch but it looks really impressive. It starts with single crochet. You can begin with fsc if you know how. Then you go into the stitch pattern!

How to Make a Shell Stitch:

  1. Yarn Over: Yarn over (YO) and insert your hook into the stitch or space where you want to create the shell.
  2. Work 5 Double Crochets: Yarn over again, pull up a loop (you'll now have 3 loops on your hook). Yarn over and pull through the first two loops, then yarn over again and pull through the remaining two loops. Repeat this process 5 times into the same stitch or space to create a shell of 5 double crochets.
  3. Complete the Shell: After completing the 5 double crochets in the same stitch or space, your shell is finished.
  4. Continue as Directed: You can repeat the shell stitch in the following rows, depending on your pattern instructions. If working a pattern, shells may be spaced with chain stitches or worked into different parts of the row (such as skipping stitches between shells).

















Primrose Stitch

Learn how to crochet the primrose stitch with this simple tutorial. It uses chain stitches and the double crochet stitch. There are few crochet stitches as beautiful as this one. Use it to dress up childrenswear patterns or make a delicate washcloth set.

How to Make a Primrose Stitch:

Start with a Foundation Chain:
- Begin by making a foundation chain with a multiple of 3 + 2 extra stitches.
- For example, chain 14 (which is 12 + 2) to create your starting row.
Row 1 (Foundation Row):
- Turn your work after making the foundation chain.
- In the second chain from your hook, work 1 double crochet (dc).
- In each following chain, work 1 double crochet across the row.
At the end of the row, you will have a row of double crochets.
Row 2 (Creating the Primrose Stitch):
- Start with a ch 3 (this counts as your first double crochet).
- Work a puff stitch into the next stitch. (To make a puff stitch: Yarn over, insert your hook into the stitch, yarn over again, and pull up a loop.
Repeat this step 3 more times so you have 5 loops on the hook, then yarn over and pull through all 5 loops).
- Ch 2 to separate the puff stitch.
- Skip 2 stitches and work a puff stitch into the next stitch.
- Repeat the puff stitch and chain 2 separation across the row.
- End the row with a ch 3 turning chain, ready to turn for the next row.
Row 3 (Repeat the Pattern):
- Turn your work and repeat Row 2 by working puff stitches into the spaces between the puff stitches from the previous row.
Each time you repeat the row, you will continue to build the flower-like pattern with the puff stitches separated by chain spaces.
